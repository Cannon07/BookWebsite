In this chapter, we generalize the discussion about the three
dimensional Schrodinger's equation to a discussion about Hamiltonians
for molecules. The molecular Hamiltonian models the quantum mechanical
energy of the electrons and nuclei for a molecule. As we learned in
[\[chap:position_and_momentum\]](#chap:position_and_momentum)\{reference-type="ref+label"
reference="chap:position_and_momentum"\}, quantum mechanical models of a
system typically proceed by starting from a classical model and then
quantizing it.

Let's suppose that we have a system with $M$ electrons and $N$ nuclei.
We can write the classical energy as

$$\begin\{aligned\}
    E = \frac\{1\}\{2\} m_e \sum_\{i=1\}^M \|v_i\|^2 + \frac\{1\}\{2\} \sum_\{j=1\}^N m_\{j\} \|V_j\|^2 + \sum_\{i=1\}^M \sum_\{j=1\}^N \frac\{Z_i e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|r_i - R_j\|\} + \frac\{1\}\{2\}\sum_\{i=1\}^M \sum_\{j\neq i\}^M \frac\{e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|r_i - r_j\|\}  + \frac\{1\}\{2\} \sum_\{i=1\}^N \sum_\{j\neq i\}^N \frac\{e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|R_i - R_j\|\}  
\end\{aligned\}$$

Here $m_e$ is the mass of an electron, and $m_j$ is the mass of the
$j$-th nucleus. $r_i$ is the position of the $i$-th electron and $R_j$
the position of the $j$-the nucleus. Note here we use the convention
that capital variables are used for nuclei position and momentum
modelling and lower case variables for electron position and momentum
modelling. $Z_i$ denotes a nuclear charge.

We apply our usual quantization rules to this formula to derive the
molecular hamiltonian

$$\begin\{aligned\}
    H &= - \frac\{\hbar\}\{2m_e\}\sum_\{i=1\}^M \nabla^2_\{r_i\} - \sum_\{j=1\}^N \frac\{\hbar\}\{2m_j\} \nabla^2_\{R_j\} 
    + \sum_\{i=1\}^M\sum_\{j=1\}^N \frac\{Z_i e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|r_i-R_j\|\} + \frac\{1\}\{2\} \sum_\{i=1\}^M \sum_\{j\neq i\}^M \frac\{e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|r_i - r_j\|\} + \frac\{1\}\{2\} \sum_\{i=1\}^N \sum_\{j\neq i\}^N \frac\{e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|R_i - R_j\|\} 
\end\{aligned\}$$

The quantization of the kinetic energy terms should be familiar from our
earlier discussion of quantization rules, but you may be confused about
why the pairwise interaction terms appear the same between the classical
energy and the quantum hamiltonian. This notation is in fact a common
shorthand defining an operator. The Coulomb operator is defined as
follows $$\begin\{aligned\}
\frac\{1\}\{|r|\} &: L^2(\mathbb\{R\}) \mapsto L^2(\mathbb\{R\}) \\
\frac\{1\}\{|r|\}(\psi) &= \frac\{1\}\{|r|\}\psi
\end\{aligned\}$$ That is, the Columb operator multiplies the wave
function by $\frac\{1\}\{|r|\}$. The Coulomb interaction terms above should
be interpreted in this same light.

## Born-Oppenheimer Approximation

The Born-Oppenheimer approximation is a widely used approximation to the
full Molecular Hamiltonian that separates the accepts the convention
that the electronic effects have limited effect on the nuclear parts of
the Hamiltonian. In this approximation, we simplify the Hamiltonian to
remove the nuclear terms

$$\begin\{aligned\}
    H &= - \frac\{\hbar\}\{2m_e\}\sum_\{i=1\}^M \nabla^2_\{r_i\} 
    + \sum_\{i=1\}^M\sum_\{j=1\}^N \frac\{Z_i e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|r_i-R_j\|\} + \frac\{1\}\{2\} \sum_\{i=1\}^M \sum_\{j\neq i\}^M \frac\{e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|r_i - r_j\|\} + \frac\{1\}\{2\} \sum_\{i=1\}^N \sum_\{j\neq i\}^N \frac\{e^2\}\{4 \pi \epsilon \|R_i - R_j\|\} 
\end\{aligned\}$$
